Introduction

The COVID-19 pandemic has had lingering consequences since 2020. Key to the combat against this pandemic was the rollout of vaccines which had early access for healthcare workers in December 2020 but later became available to the general public in April 2021. Prior to the vaccines, management was key in preventing the spread of COVID and varied from state to state. This analysis aims to determine the difference in COVID-19 test positivity rates and death rates between Ohio and California during 2020 until the point when vaccines were distributed. By comparing these states trends with the national average, this analysis seeks to understand how both states managed the pandemic before the vaccines were made available and whether their approaches led to different outcomes.

Research Question

How did Ohio and California compare in their COVID-19 positive test rates and death rates prior to the widespread availability of vaccines, and how do these trends compare with national averages? To what extent did government intervention levels influence these patterns prior to vaccines?

Methods

The datasets are publicly available on www.covidtracking.com/data/download. They provide daily updates on COVID-19 statistics from all of the United States as well as Ohio and California. These variables measured include test positivity rates, death counts, hospitilizations, and other COVID-related metrics. The data ends March 2021 just prior to when vaccines became publicly available in April 2021. For population estimates, the US Census Bureau was used for their annual state population estimates for 2020 to calculate death rates.

Data from Oxford COVID-19 Government Response Tracker (OxCGRT) was obtained that provides systematic data on government responses to the COVID-19 pandemic. This database included indicators such as a measure of the stringency of government policies and health containment measures across different regions of the world and between states in the USA. The data was initially in long format but was converted to wide format for use. This was then merged with the previous COVID-19 databases for Ohio and California.

stringency_data <- read_excel("C:\\Users\\patri\\OneDrive\\Documents\\MPH Sem 3\\Health Data Science\\PM566-Project\\Midterm Data\\OxCGRT_timeseries_all_v1.xlsx", sheet = "StringencyIndex")
containment_data <- read_excel("C:\\Users\\patri\\OneDrive\\Documents\\MPH Sem 3\\Health Data Science\\PM566-Project\\Midterm Data\\OxCGRT_timeseries_all_v1.xlsx", sheet = "ContainmentHealthIndex")

date_cols_stringency <- grep("^\\d{2}[A-Za-z]{3}\\d{4}$", colnames(stringency_data), value = TRUE)
date_cols_containment <- grep("^\\d{2}[A-Za-z]{3}\\d{4}$", colnames(containment_data), value = TRUE)

start_date <- "04Mar2020"
end_date <- "07Mar2021"

stringency_filtered <- stringency_data %>%
  select(CountryCode, RegionCode, RegionName, all_of(date_cols_stringency[which(date_cols_stringency >= start_date & date_cols_stringency <= end_date)]))

containment_filtered <- containment_data %>%
  select(CountryCode, RegionCode, RegionName, all_of(date_cols_containment[which(date_cols_containment >= start_date & date_cols_containment <= end_date)]))

stringency_long <- stringency_filtered %>%
  pivot_longer(
    cols = all_of(date_cols_stringency[which(date_cols_stringency >= start_date & date_cols_stringency <= end_date)]),
    names_to = "date",
    values_to = "StringencyIndex"
  ) %>%
  mutate(date = as.Date(date, format = "%d%b%Y"))

containment_long <- containment_filtered %>%
  pivot_longer(
    cols = all_of(date_cols_containment[which(date_cols_containment >= start_date & date_cols_containment <= end_date)]),
    names_to = "date",
    values_to = "ContainmentHealthIndex"
  ) %>%
  mutate(date = as.Date(date, format = "%d%b%Y"))

indices_long <- stringency_long %>%
  left_join(containment_long, by = c("CountryCode", "RegionCode", "RegionName", "date"), relationship="many-to-many")

Removing Rows with Missing or Implausible Values

Rows with missing or implausible values (positivity rates > 100%) were adjusted accordingly.

california_filtered <- california_data %>%
  filter(!is.na(date) & !is.na(positivity_rate))

ohio_filtered <- ohio_data %>%
  filter(!is.na(date) & !is.na(positivity_rate) & totalTestResultsIncrease > 0) %>%
  mutate(positiveIncrease = ifelse(positiveIncrease > totalTestResultsIncrease, totalTestResultsIncrease, positiveIncrease)) %>%
  mutate(positivity_rate = (positiveIncrease / totalTestResultsIncrease) * 100) %>%
  filter(positivity_rate <= 100)

national_filtered <- national_data %>%
  filter(!is.na(date) & !is.na(positivity_rate) & totalTestResultsIncrease > 0) %>%
  mutate(positiveIncrease = ifelse(positiveIncrease > totalTestResultsIncrease, totalTestResultsIncrease, positiveIncrease)) %>%
  mutate(positivity_rate = (positiveIncrease / totalTestResultsIncrease) * 100) %>%
  filter(positivity_rate <= 100)

Conclusion

This analysis highlights key differences in how California and Ohio managed COVID-19 positivity and death rates in 2020, leading up to the widespread availability of vaccines. Initially, California experienced higher positivity rates, particularly from April to October 2020, while Ohio saw a notable rise around November 2020, as depicted in Figure 1. Ohio consistently maintained a higher death rate compared to California, except for a brief period between January and February 2021. Both states demonstrated distinct trends relative to the national average, likely reflecting differences in state-specific reopening policies and intervention levels.

Government intervention levels played a significant role in these trends. Californias consistently high stringency and containment measures may have contributed to initially lower death rates as seen in Figure 6. However, these measures showed limited effectiveness during the winter surge, when positivity (Figure 4) and death rates spiked. Ohios response was more variable, with containment measures fluctuating in response to death rates (Figure 5) and positivity rates (Figure 3). This reactive approach may have allowed for increased spread, especially during the winter months when indoor gatherings became more common.

Overall, Californias early, consistent restrictions may have helped mitigate the spread initially, while Ohios more flexible policies provided temporary control. Both states faced significant challenges during the holiday season, suggesting that social behaviors and seasonal factors played a critical role in transmission rates, even amid government interventions. These findings underscore the importance of sustained, adaptable public health strategies to manage pandemic spread and protect public health effectively.
